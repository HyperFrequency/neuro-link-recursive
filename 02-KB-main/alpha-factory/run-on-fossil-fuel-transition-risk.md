# A Run on Fossil Fuel? Climate Change and Transition Risk

## Overview

Barnett (2024) analyses dynamic general-equilibrium implications of [[climate-transition risk]] on macro outcomes and asset prices. A central result: expected fossil-fuel restrictions can produce a [[run on fossil fuel]] — accelerated production and falling spot prices — or a [[reverse run]] with restrained production and rising prices. Outcome depends on expected transition-shock consequences and existing climate policy. Fossil-fuel firm prices decline in both cases. Empirical measure of climate-transition-risk innovations shows dynamics consistent with a run.

## Conceptual Model

Standard [[Hotelling]] intuition for exhaustible resources: expected future scarcity implies rising prices (cost-of-carry for extraction). Barnett inverts: if the resource risks becoming [[stranded]] (regulation / carbon tax destroys future value), owners race to extract now — a "run." Dynamic-equilibrium model formalises this under uncertainty over transition timing.

## Details

**Model structure** (inferred from abstract):
- Representative fossil-fuel firm chooses extraction path maximising discounted profits.
- Government/regulator unpredictably imposes transition (tax, ban).
- Households + production use fossil inputs; substitution to clean technology is costly.
- General-equilibrium clearing determines spot and forward prices.

**Run vs reverse run**:
- [[Run]] scenario: severe expected future regulation + limited substitution ⇒ firms race to extract, accelerating supply, depressing spot prices. Firm valuations fall (future cash flows destroyed).
- [[Reverse run]]: if transition is anticipated to be mild / substitution is easy, firms restrain production, preserving reserves; spot prices rise.
- Both equilibria are possible; selection depends on expectations and policy.

**Firm-price implication** unified: spot price up or down, but expected present value of fossil-fuel firms falls in both cases.

**Empirical strategy**:
- Constructs novel [[transition-risk proxy]] (likely via textual analysis of policy documents, legislative events, or climate-related shocks on financial markets).
- Uses [[SVAR]] or local-projection to trace dynamic responses of oil prices, fossil firm equity, production measures.
- Finds responses consistent with run dynamics.

**Competing literature**:
- [[Bolton-Kacperczyk]] (2021, 2022): carbon premium in stock returns — riskier carbon firms earn higher returns.
- [[Engle et al.]] (2020): hedging climate risk via text-based mimicking portfolios.
- [[Pastor-Stambaugh-Taylor]] (2021): green assets outperform with unexpected ESG shocks.

**Policy implications**:
- Credible transition policies shape which equilibrium realises.
- Gradual carbon taxes with [[forward guidance]] may induce smoother dynamics (reverse run) vs sudden bans (run).
